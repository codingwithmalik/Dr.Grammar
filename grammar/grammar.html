The Noun â€“ Kinds of Nouns

A Noun is a word used as the name of a person, place, or thing.

Note: The word thing is used to mean anything that we can think of.

Example: Babur was a wise king.

The noun Babur refers to a particular king, so we call it a Proper Noun. The noun king could refer to any king, so it is a Common Noun.

Common Noun

Names given in common to every person or thing of the same class or kind.

Examples:

  • book, pen, apple
  • boy, girl, teacher
  • city, country, river

Sample Sentence:

The book is on the table.

Names of specific persons, places, or things.

Examples:

  • John, Mary, Einstein

  • Eiffel Tower, Taj Mahal

  • An army = a collection of soldiers.

  • A herd of cattle is passing.

THE NOUN : GENDER (Masculine / Feminine)

Understanding how nouns can be masculine, feminine, common, or neuter

You know that living beings are either the male or the female. Now compare the words in the following pairs:

Masculine

Boy - Girl

Hero - Heroine

Cock-sparrow - Hen-sparrow

Feminine

What do you notice?

The first word of each pair is the name of a male animal.

The second word of each pair is the name of a female animal.

A noun that denotes a male animal is said to be of the Masculine Gender.

A noun that denotes a female animal is said to be of the Feminine Gender.

Masculine

Male beings

Feminine

Female beings

Common

Either gender

Neuter

No gender

A noun that denotes either a male or a female is said to be of the Common Gender; as

Parent, child, friend, pupil, servant, thief, relation, enemy, cousin, person, orphan, student, baby, monarch, neighbour, infant.

A noun that denotes a thing that is neither male nor female (i.e., thing without life) is said to be of the Neuter Gender; as,

Book, pen, room, tree.

[Neuter means neither, that is, neither male nor female]

In Modern English, the Gender of a noun is entirely a matter of meaning. It has nothing to do with the form of a noun, unlike many other languages.

Objects without life are often personified, that is, spoken of as if they were living beings. We then regard them as males or females.

The Masculine Gender is applied to objects remarkable for strength and violence; as,

The Sun, Summer, Winter, Time, Death.

The sun sheds his beams on rich and poor alike.

The Feminine Gender is applied to objects remarkable for beauty, gentleness, and gracefulness; as,

The Moon, the Earth, Spring, Autumn, Nature, Liberty, Justice, Mercy, Peace, Hope, Charity.

The moon has hidden her face behind a cloud.

Spring has spread her mantle of green over the earth.

Peace hath her victories no less renowned than war.

This usage is most common in poetry, though certain nouns are personified in prose as well. A ship, for instance, is often referred to as she; as,

The ship lost all her boats in the storm.

THE NOUN: CASE (Nominative / Objective / Possessive)

Understanding how nouns function in sentences

The form of a noun that shows its relation to other words in a sentence is called its Case.

There are three cases in English:

Nominative

Subject of verb

Example: Ali is a good boy

Objective

Object of verb/preposition

Example: The teacher praised Ali

Possessive

Shows ownership

Example: Ali's book

The Nominative Case is the form of a noun when it is used as the subject of a verb; as,

Ali is a good boy.

The Objective Case is the form of a noun when it is used as the object of a verb or preposition; as,

The teacher praised Ali.

The Objective Case is also used after certain prepositions; as,

The book is on the table with Ali.

The Possessive Case is the form of a noun that shows ownership or possession; as,

Ali's book is on the table.

The Possessive Case is formed by adding an apostrophe and s to the noun; as,

Ali's book, the teacher's desk.

If the noun is plural and ends in s, only an apostrophe is added; as,

the teachers' lounge, the students' books.

If the noun is plural and does not end in s, an apostrophe and s are added; as,

the children's playground,

The Possessive Case can also be formed by using the preposition of; as,

The book of Ali is on the table.

However, in English, the possessive form (e.g., John's book) is more commonly used than the of construction (e.g., the book of John).

Adjective

An adjective is a word that describes or modifies a noun or pronoun. It provides additional information about the noun, such as its quality, quantity, size, shape, color, etc.

Adjectives can be used to compare nouns, indicate possession, or specify which noun is being referred to.

For example, in the sentence "The tall building is beautiful," the adjective "tall" describes the noun "building", and "beautiful" provides more information about the building.

Adjectives can be used in various forms, such as comparative and superlative, to compare different nouns or indicate the highest or lowest degree of a quality.

In English, adjectives can be placed before the noun they modify (e.g., "a red apple") or after a linking verb (e.g., "the apple is red").

Adjectives play a crucial role in making sentences more descriptive and vivid, allowing for clearer communication and expression of ideas.

Degrees of Adjectives

Understanding Positive, Comparative, and Superlative Degrees

Adjectives have three degrees of comparison: Positive, Comparative, and Superlative. These degrees help us compare qualities between different objects or people.

Positive Degree

The basic form of an adjective

Examples:

tall, big, happy, beautiful

Comparative Degree

Used to compare two things

Examples:

taller, bigger, happier, more beautiful

Superlative Degree

Used to compare more than two things

Examples:

tallest, biggest, happiest, most beautiful

Pronoun

A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun or noun phrase. Pronouns help avoid repetition and make sentences more concise and natural.

More About Verbs

Verbs are essential for constructing meaningful sentences. They not only show action but also indicate time (tense), mood, and voice. Verbs can be regular (forming their past tense by adding -ed, e.g., walked) or irregular (changing form completely, e.g., went from go).

  • Tense: Verbs change form to show when an action happens (past, present, future).
  • Voice: Verbs can be in the active voice (the subject does the action) or passive voice (the subject receives the action).
  • Mood: Verbs can express facts (indicative), commands (imperative), or possibilities (subjunctive).
  • Verb Phrases: Sometimes, a verb consists of more than one word (e.g., has been running).

Tip: Every sentence must have a verb, and understanding verb types helps you write and speak more clearly.

Modal Verbs: In-Depth

Modal verbs are special auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, or obligation. They are always used with the base form of the main verb and do not change form for tense or subject.

  • can/could: ability or possibility (She can swim., Could you help me?)
  • may/might: permission or possibility (You may leave., It might rain.)
  • must: necessity or strong obligation (You must wear a seatbelt.)
  • shall/should: suggestion, offer, or advice (Shall we dance?, You should study.)
  • will/would: future intention, polite request, or habitual action (I will call you., Would you like tea?)

Rules for Modal Verbs:

  • Modal verbs are not used alone; they need a main verb (e.g., She can dance).
  • They do not take -s in the third person singular (e.g., He can go, not He cans go).
  • Questions and negatives are formed without do/does/did (e.g., Can you swim?, She cannot swim.).

Common Mistakes: Using modals with another auxiliary (e.g., He does can swim âŒ). Always use only one modal or auxiliary at a time.
