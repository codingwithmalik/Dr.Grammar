Types of Sentences

By Purpose

Sentences can be classified based on their purpose. The four main types are: Declarative, Interrogative, Imperative, and Exclamatory.

Declarative

A sentence that makes a statement or expresses an idea.

Example:

The sun rises in the east.

Click for more info

Interrogative

A sentence that asks a question.

Example:

Where are you going?

Click for more info

Imperative

A sentence that gives a command, request, or advice.

Example:

Please close the door.

Click for more info

Exclamatory

A sentence that expresses strong emotion or surprise.

Example:

What a beautiful day!

By Structure

Sentences can also be classified based on their structure. The four main types are: Simple, Compound, Complex, and Compound-Complex sentences.

Simple Sentence

Contains a single independent clause and expresses a complete thought.

Example:

She reads every morning.

Click for more info

Compound Sentence

Contains two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (and, but, or, etc.).

Example:

She reads every morning, and he writes at night.

Click for more info

Complex Sentence

Contains one independent clause and at least one dependent (subordinate) clause.

Example:

Although she was tired, she finished her homework.

Click for more info

Compound-Complex Sentence

Contains at least two independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

Example:

She finished her homework, and he cooked dinner because they had guests.

More About Adverb Phrases

Adverb phrases modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs, providing more detail about how, when, where, or to what extent something happens.

  • They often answer questions like: How? When? Where? To what extent?
Examples:
  • She spoke in a whisper. (How?)
  • We will meet after the movie. (When?)
  • He ran to the store. (Where?)
  • They worked with great enthusiasm. (To what extent?)

Types of Clauses

Clauses are groups of words that contain a subject and a verb. They can be independent (able to stand alone as a sentence) or dependent (cannot stand alone). Here are the main types:

Main (Independent) Clause

A clause that can stand alone as a complete sentence.

Example:

Difference Between Dependent and Independent Clauses

Independent (Main) Clause: Can stand alone as a complete sentence. It expresses a complete thought and has both a subject and a verb.

Example: She finished her homework.

Dependent (Subordinate) Clause: Cannot stand alone as a sentence. It depends on an independent clause to complete its meaning. Usually begins with words like because, if, when, etc.

Example: because she was tired

Tip: If a group of words can be a sentence by itself, it's an independent clause. If it leaves you waiting for more information, it's dependent!

Tenses

Tenses show the time of action or state of being in a sentence. English has three main tenses: Present, Past, and Future. Each tense can be further divided into four aspects: Simple, Continuous, Perfect, and Perfect Continuous.

Present

Actions happening now, habits, or general truths.

Quick Facts: Most common tense in English. Used for routines, facts, and ongoing actions.
Example 1: She walks to school.
Example 2: The sun rises in the east.
Common Mistake: Forgetting to add 's' or 'es' for he/she/it in the simple present.
Click for more details

Past

Actions that happened before now.

Quick Facts: Used for completed actions, stories, and historical events.
Example 1: She walked to school yesterday.
Example 2: They played football last week.
Common Mistake: Using the wrong past form for irregular verbs (e.g., 'goed' instead of 'went').
Click for more details

Future

Actions that will happen after now.

Quick Facts: Expresses predictions, plans, and promises. Several ways to form the future in English.
Example 1: She will walk to school.
Example 2: I am going to visit my friend.
Usage Tip: Use 'will' for predictions, 'going to' for plans, and present continuous for scheduled events.

Present Tense Types

Present Indefinite (Simple Present)

Describes habitual actions, general truths, or facts.

Structure: Subject + V1 (base form/s/es)
Formation:
  • Add -s/-es to verb with he/she/it
  • Use base form for I/you/we/they
  • Add -es after s, sh, ch, x, o
Examples:
  • She walks to school. (Positive)
  • They do not play tennis. (Negative)
  • Does he study French? (Question)
Click for more details

Present Continuous

Describes actions happening right now or temporary situations.

Structure: Subject + am/is/are + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Add -ing to base verb
  • Double final consonant if CVC pattern
  • Drop final -e before -ing
Examples:
  • I am reading a book. (Positive)
  • She is not working now. (Negative)
  • Are they playing football? (Question)
Click for more details

Present Perfect

Describes actions that happened at an unspecified time before now or have relevance to the present.

Structure: Subject + have/has + V3 (past participle)
Formation:
  • Use 'has' with he/she/it
  • Use 'have' with I/you/we/they
  • Past participle forms vary
Examples:
  • I have finished my work. (Positive)
  • She has not seen the movie. (Negative)
  • Have they arrived yet? (Question)
Click for more details

Present Perfect Continuous

Describes actions that started in the past and are still continuing or have recently stopped.

Structure: Subject + have/has + been + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Use 'has been' with he/she/it
  • Use 'have been' with I/you/we/they
  • Follow -ing form rules
Examples:
  • I have been working for hours. (Positive)
  • She has not been sleeping well. (Negative)
  • Have they been waiting long? (Question)
Click for more details

Past Tense Types

Past Indefinite (Simple Past)

Describes actions that happened at a specific time in the past.

Structure: Subject + V2 (past form)
Formation:
  • Regular verbs: add -ed
  • Irregular verbs: unique past forms
  • Same form for all subjects
Examples:
  • She walked to school. (Positive)
  • They did not go to the party. (Negative)
  • Did he finish his homework? (Question)
Click for more details

Past Continuous

Describes actions that were ongoing at a particular time in the past.

Structure: Subject + was/were + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Use 'was' with I/he/she/it
  • Use 'were' with you/we/they
  • Follow -ing form rules
Examples:
  • I was reading when you called. (Positive)
  • They were not listening. (Negative)
  • Was she working late? (Question)
Click for more details

Past Perfect

Describes actions that were completed before another action in the past.

Structure: Subject + had + V3 (past participle)
Formation:
  • Use 'had' for all subjects
  • Past participle forms vary
  • Often used with 'before' or 'by the time'
Examples:
  • She had finished before I arrived. (Positive)
  • They had not left when I came. (Negative)
  • Had you seen him before? (Question)
Click for more details

Past Perfect Continuous

Describes actions that were ongoing in the past up to a certain point.

Structure: Subject + had + been + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Use 'had been' for all subjects
  • Follow -ing form rules
  • Often used to show duration before another past action
Examples:
  • I had been working for hours when she called. (Positive)
  • They had not been living there long. (Negative)
  • Had you been waiting long? (Question)
Click for more details

Future Tense Types

Future Indefinite (Simple Future)

Describes actions that will happen at a later time.

Structure: Subject + will/shall + V1
Formation:
  • Use 'will' for all subjects (more common)
  • 'Shall' traditionally used with I/we (formal)
  • Base form of verb for all subjects
Examples:
  • I will help you tomorrow. (Positive)
  • They will not attend the meeting. (Negative)
  • Will she come to the party? (Question)
Click for more details

Future Continuous

Describes actions that will be ongoing at a specific time in the future.

Structure: Subject + will/shall + be + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Use 'will be' for all subjects
  • Follow -ing form rules
  • Often used for actions in progress at a future time
Examples:
  • This time tomorrow, I will be flying to Paris. (Positive)
  • They will not be working late. (Negative)
  • Will you be staying long? (Question)
Click for more details

Future Perfect

Describes actions that will be completed before a certain time in the future.

Structure: Subject + will/shall + have + V3
Formation:
  • Use 'will have' for all subjects
  • Past participle forms vary
  • Often used with 'by' or 'before' + future time
Examples:
  • By next week, I will have finished the project. (Positive)
  • They will not have arrived by then. (Negative)
  • Will you have completed it by Friday? (Question)
Click for more details

Future Perfect Continuous

Describes actions that will be ongoing up to a certain point in the future.

Structure: Subject + will/shall + have + been + V1 + ing
Formation:
  • Use 'will have been' for all subjects
  • Follow -ing form rules
  • Often used to emphasize duration up to a future point
Examples:
  • By June, I will have been working here for ten years. (Positive)
  • They will not have been studying long enough. (Negative)
  • Will you have been waiting long? (Question)

What is a Tense?

Tense is the form of a verb that shows the time of an action or state. English has three main tenses:

  • Present: Now, current actions, or general truths.
  • Past: Actions that happened before now.
  • Future: Actions that will happen after now.

Tip: Each tense can be simple, continuous, perfect, or perfect continuous. For example, "She is walking" (Present Continuous), "She had walked" (Past Perfect).
